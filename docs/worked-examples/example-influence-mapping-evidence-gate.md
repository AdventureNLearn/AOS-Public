# Worked Example: Influence Mapping + Evidence Gate

This example shows how to combine the Influence & Organizational Mapping Framework with Evidence Gate for rigorous analysis.

## Query
"Map the key organizations and individuals influencing a specific state-level education policy debate, and assess the strength of evidence behind claims of coordinated influence."

## Step 1: Apply Influence Mapping Framework
Using the structured mapping approach:

**Scope:** Major organizations and individuals shaping a recent state education policy debate (last 3 years).

**Key Entities Identified:**
- Several education nonprofits and advocacy groups
- Major donors and foundations
- State legislators and agency officials
- Media outlets and commentators

**Key Relationships Mapped:**
- Multiple overlapping board memberships and personnel
- Coordinated grantmaking from a small number of foundations
- Aligned public messaging and report releases

**Influence Observations:**
- A relatively small network of funders and organizations appears to have outsized influence on the policy debate framing.

**Disclosure Patterns:**
- Some relationships are publicly disclosed, while others require deeper document review to uncover.

**Confidence Levels:**
- Core funding and personnel overlaps: High
- Coordinated narrative strategy: Medium to High (strong patterns, some direct evidence)

## Step 2: Apply Evidence Gate
All major claims labeled:

- Funding overlaps between specific foundations and advocacy groups → **SOURCED** (public grant records and tax filings)
- Coordinated messaging across organizations → **INFERRED** (timing and content alignment is clear, but direct coordination evidence is partial)
- Claims of “astroturfing” or fully manufactured grassroots support → **SPECULATIVE** (patterns exist but direct evidence of fabrication is limited)

## Final Synthesized Output
A relatively concentrated network of funders and organizations has significantly shaped the public debate around this education policy. While some coordination is visible through funding and personnel flows, stronger evidence would be needed to substantiate claims of fully orchestrated narrative control. The combination of mapping and evidence labeling provides a clearer picture than either method alone.

Key Takeaway:
Influence Mapping revealed structural relationships. Evidence Gate then forced disciplined labeling of what could actually be supported versus what remained speculative. This produces more credible and defensible civic research.
